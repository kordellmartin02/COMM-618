This is an interesting piece on the correlation between gas prices and presidential approval ratings. It’s a great story idea since the war caused national gas prices to rise to or above $4, and President Trump boasted about gas prices being $2.30 at the State of the Union in February. The graphs from each dataset are easy to follow, but I was looking for more specifics. They could have mentioned the lowest or highest gas prices in each presidency and how that may have caused a corresponding presidential approval rating, with them also providing the specific number for that as well. The article's length served its purpose and didn’t drag on long enough for me to get turned off from reading about the economy. They did mention how the correlation has become weaker since the Obama administration, but gas prices during that time were significantly high. It would have been good to see why voters tended to keep a steady approval rating of former President Obama while gas was well above $5. This could have been applied to the first Trump administration as well, since gas prices never touched the $4 mark, while he had a lower approval rating than most presidents. It was mentioned near the end of the article, however, I would’ve liked it if they expanded on why voters don’t necessarily care about the price at the pump when supporting the U.S.-Israeli war in Iran. That seems interesting, yet it's only mentioned in two sentences.
