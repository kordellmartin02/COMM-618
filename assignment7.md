Data from California shows that robberies in Los Angeles, CA, had been decreasing since 2006, as documented in 2013. Reports for that year show that robberies had been at their lowest point since the beginning of the 20th century. What I’m noticing is the slight increase in robberies in 2006 that stopped a noticeable decrease in the early 2000s. Though there had been a steady rise starting in 2005, the numbers increased by more than 1,000 robberies in 2006. After, the robbery rate began to fall again, matching the decline that Los Angeles had started in 2002. Also, it is noticeable that the number of robberies in the city in 2001 was cut in half by 2013.

The decline in robberies after 2006 could be a consequence of the Los Angeles Police Department operating under a federal consent decree that restricted how police conducted oversight in the city. The decree was initiated in 2001 and ended in 2013, and enforced officers with stricter codes to follow when stopping and searching citizens. Despite an uptick in the mid-2000s, robbery did decrease under the decree. What could the LAPD have done during the duration of the decree that decreased violent crime and cut robberies by almost half, while also having less freedom to police?

The data is trustworthy because of its precise documentation of all violent crime in California by city and county, and its thoroughness in detailing sections of Los Angeles and their crime rates. The dataset was published by the California Department of Public Health, and particular data on violent crime was collected by the Federal Bureau of Investigation. The California Department of Public Health also used the data to conduct unbiased studies that divided violent crime statistics by demographic, explaining how some demographics are more impacted by crime than others.
